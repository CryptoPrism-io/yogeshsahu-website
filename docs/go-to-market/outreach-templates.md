# Outreach Templates

## Positioning Rules

Use these rules consistently:

- lead with execution pressure, not the stack
- sell hands-on architecture leadership, not implementation bandwidth
- present the diagnostic as the first paid step
- make the buyer feel the cost of a wrong full-time hire
- avoid low-budget or commodity language

## Reply When Someone Asks "What do you do exactly?"

I step in as a hands-on chief solutions architect / fractional CTO when a business needs more than execution capacity.

Usually the pattern is AI pressure, harder architecture decisions, and just enough ambiguity in scope or delivery to slow the business down.

I help make the next decisions obvious without the company having to commit to a full-time CTO hire too early.

## Reply When Someone Asks "Why not just hire an engineer?"

If the issue is implementation bandwidth, hiring an engineer is right.

If the issue is technical judgment, AI direction, architecture tradeoffs, discovery quality, or cross-functional alignment, more implementation capacity will not fix the real problem.

That is the gap I fill.

## Reply When Someone Asks "Why not hire a full-time CTO?"

Sometimes that is the right move.

But markets are moving fast, and a wrong full-time technical hire is expensive to unwind. Fractional leadership lets the business get senior judgment and flexibility before taking on fixed-cost commitment.

## Discovery Call Framing

Use these lines in calls and messages:

- "I am most useful when the problem is not simple implementation capacity."
- "The first goal is to make the next technical decisions obvious."
- "I help product and engineering operate from the same technical reality."
- "AI is usually not the problem. Unclear AI direction is."
